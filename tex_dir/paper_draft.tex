It is well established that Earth's atmospheric temperatures are seasonal.  
Aside from the four standard seasons associated with Earth's axial tilt, long-range seasonal temperatures are influenced by ocean temperatures and myriad minute details of Earth's orbit (called Milankovitch cycles) \cite{milankovitch1941canon}.
While understanding these cycles is still an open area of investigation, they typically act in predictable ways over long periods of time.  
Within the last 200 years, however, Earth's climate has changed faster than in any time known since the mass extinction event of the dinosaurs \cite{mulkern2013climatechange}.

With the climate changes on Earth trending toward another potential mass extinction event, there is great interest in identifying which factors have caused such a rapid change in global climate.
Recently, there has been increasing momentum in both scientific and societal circles suggesting that Earth's rapid shift in temperature is largely due to a rise in atmospheric CO$_2$ levels \cite{foote1856art, arrhenius1896xxxi, callendar1938artificial, keeling1960concentration, manabe1967thermal, charney1979carbon, santer1996search}.

In the interest of seeking effective models for climate change, we consider several distinct model architectures operating under varying assumptions.

\subsection{Mixture Models}
One core assumption that many models hold is that all data was generated by the same system. 
Mixture Models avoid this assumption.  
Instead, they expect multiple models to be fit to different portions of the data.
This allows for the possibility that the system was replaced or fundamentally altered somewhere in the training data, which modified system will most influence data in the near future.
The fundamental thesis of climate change is that the climate is directly and fundamentally changing, whether due to human impact or not.
As such, it seems prudent to drop the assumption of homogeneous generation.

Gaussian Mixture Models are one form of Mixture Model. 
They operate by assuming that the data was generated by multiple multivariate Gaussian distributions, and assign membership likelihood of data according to the probability densities of the components at their locations \cite{pearson1894iii}.
They are not often used on sequential, dependent time series, as another common assumption of their usage is that the data being fit are independent and identically distributed according to the fit variables.
They are often biased to assume a split close to the center of the data temporally, as being a clear location for division of the data.
However, they may still be used for modeling of time-series data, even incorporating time as a fit variable, leveraging assumptions of group membership and proximity similarity for identification of distinct processes generating the data.
We will fit a Gaussian Mixture Model with two components, for simplicity assuming there is at most one fundamental shift, and then fit a linear regression to the data for each Gaussian component for explainability in the shifts of climate behavior.

\subsection{Kalman Filter}

\par The world is a large space, making it impossible to have complete observations of temperature, sea ice levels or CO$_2$ emissions globally.
Moreover, new data streams in continuously as temperatures, seasons and even human activity fluctuate.
This leads the Kalman filter to be a natural choice in modeling global average temperature trends.

\par As observations are limited, the Kalman filter's natural smoothing of observations enables us to gain a much clearer understanding of the evolution of temperature trends over time.
Moreover, the Kalman filter comfortably handles dynamic systems (like climate data) in updating its estimates and forecasts.
The Kalman filter provides the statistically optimal estimate and is especially effective when dealing with Gaussian noise -- which is a fair assumption to make for the noise of our global observations.

\par By pairing the Kalman filter with other methods, such as ARIMA (see Subsection \ref{met:ARIMA}), we may use exogenous data (e.g., CO$_2$ emissions) to make plausible forecasts for future global average measurements.

\subsection{Seasonal Time Series}
\label{met:seasonal}
Another valuable tool for analyzing the overall temperature trend is a time series decomposition.
This process involves decomposing a single observation series into three components:  (1) a moving average of the data, (2) a seasonal variation (i.e., known temperature changes over the course of a calendar year) and (3) a remainder.
To observe global long-term trends that extend across decades and centuries, we examine the world average temperatures over the last 200+ years \cite{berkeleyearth_temperature}.
After computing a moving average, we compute the seasonal average with the detrended data.
Finally, we compute the remainder to verify the accuracy of the seasonal trends identified.

\subsection{ARIMA Models}
\label{met:ARIMA}
\par ARIMA models use past time series data to forecast future predictions. It does so by using past values, past forecasting errors, and taking differences of consecutive terms to stabilize the mean of the time series. The general formula for an ARIMA$(p, d, q)$ model is given by
\[
\Delta^d Z_t = \mathbf{c} + \left (\sum_{i=1}^p \Phi_i \mathbf{y}_{t- i} \right ) + \left (\sum_{j=0}^q \Theta_j \mathbf{\epsilon}_{t - j} \right ).
\]

\par ARIMA models can be used for multivariate data, finding correlations between different features. For instance, we could examine correlations between temperature and CO$_2$ levels, or between CO$_2$ levels and ice levels.

\par ARIMA models are powerful because their coefficients are easy to interpret, yielding correlation between past measurements and current ones. Similarly, we can use ARIMA models to predict future states, enabling us to cast present results into the future.

\par These models, while powerful, are further improved by decomposing time series, as detailed in Subsection \ref{met:seasonal}.  Determining seasonal components and incorporating these into ARIMA models enable them to have more predictive power, since seasonal components do not interfere with general trends, or vice versa. These models, called SARIMA models, extend the ARIMA model framework.

\section{Conclusion} 
% Describe the final conclusions that you draw from your computations, results, and analysis. 

\par From our combined analysis, we find that Earth's average temperature has continuously and rapidly trended upward over the last 75 years.
  Our models indicate this trend will continue without significant intervention, particularly in human CO$_2$ emissions. Similarly, we found that volcanoes produced less than 1\% of the amount of CO$_2$ that humans have in the time intervals that we examined, implying that they are likely not the cause of significant climate change.

\par According to our analysis, US average temperatures are forecast to reach 56 degrees Fahrenheit by 2100 at our current trajectory (see Figure~\ref{fig:sarimax_avg_temp}) -- marking a 4.5-degree increase from 1950.
  Contrast this with the Kalman forecast for declining CO$_2$ emissions (see Figure~\ref{fig:Kalman_Exog}).
  With just a 3\% annual reduction in CO$_2$ emissions, we find US average temperatures are forecast to return below 52 degrees Fahrenheit (consistent with average temperatures during the early 20th-century) within a five-decade timespan.

\par In short, our analysis paints a clear picture: reducing carbon emissions may effectively reverse the unprecedented increase in global average temperatures.
  However, without these reduction efforts, average temperatures will continue to rise further and further beyond their historical baseline.
  The global climate outcomes of future decades are heavily governed by our emissions today, only further underscoring the critical importance we face today as we respond to nature's call for help.
